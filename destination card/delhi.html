Delhi

India's Capital - Where History Meets Modernity

About Delhi

Delhi, the capital of India, is a city where ancient history seamlessly blends with modern urban life. This vibrant metropolis has been the seat of power for numerous empires and dynasties throughout history, making it one of the most historically significant cities in India. From the grandeur of Mughal architecture to the bustling markets of Old Delhi, the city offers an unparalleled experience of India's rich cultural heritage.

Today, Delhi stands as a symbol of India's progress and diversity. The city is a perfect amalgamation of old and new - ancient monuments coexist with modern skyscrapers, traditional bazaars share space with luxury malls, and authentic street food delights food lovers alongside fine dining restaurants. Whether you're a history enthusiast, a food lover, or a shopaholic, Delhi has something extraordinary to offer everyone.

Rich History

Delhi's history spans over 2,500 years, with evidence of continuous habitation since the 6th century BCE. The city has been destroyed and rebuilt multiple times, earning it the nickname "the city of seven cities" referring to the various historical capitals built in the Delhi region.

The most significant period in Delhi's history began in the 12th century with the establishment of Delhi Sultanate. Qutub Minar, built by Qutb-ud-din Aibak in 1193, marks the beginning of Islamic rule in India. This 73-meter tall minaret is one of the tallest brick minarets in the world and remains an iconic symbol of Delhi.

In the 16th century, Humayun, the second Mughal emperor, established his capital in Delhi and built the magnificent Humayun's Tomb - the first garden-tomb on the Indian subcontinent. This architectural marvel, built in 1570, later inspired the construction of the Taj Mahal. The tomb is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and showcases the finest example of Mughal architecture.

The Red Fort, built by Shah Jahan when he moved the capital back to Delhi in 1639, served as the main residence of the Mughal emperors for nearly 200 years. This massive red sandstone fort, also a UNESCO World Heritage Site, witnessed many significant events in Indian history, including India's independence celebrations.
